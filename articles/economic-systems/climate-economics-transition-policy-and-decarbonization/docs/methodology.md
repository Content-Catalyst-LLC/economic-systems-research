# Methodology

## Purpose

This article companion models climate economics, transition policy, and decarbonization as interacting emissions, sectoral transition, investment, policy-mix, carbon-lock-in, adaptation, finance, justice, international equity, and implementation systems.

The workflow:

1. Creates stylized sector emissions and transition scenarios.
2. Calculates emissions, carbon intensity, energy intensity, and decarbonization rates.
3. Models policy packages combining pricing, regulation, public investment, industrial policy, and social protection.
4. Scores transition investment using public investment, private capital, policy credibility, and cost-of-capital support.
5. Models carbon lock-in using capital-stock life, infrastructure dependence, incumbent power, and replacement readiness.
6. Evaluates climate damages through temperature stress, exposure, vulnerability, and adaptation capacity.
7. Scores just-transition conditions for labor, regions, income support, public services, and local investment.
8. Evaluates hard-to-abate sectors using technological readiness, process emissions, energy needs, and procurement leverage.
9. Evaluates global burden-sharing using historic responsibility, current emissions, fiscal capacity, vulnerability, and development need.
10. Exports tables, figures, and a SQLite database.
11. Replicates the logic across Python, R, Stata, SQL, and Julia.

## Key Concepts

- Climate change is an economic system risk, not a narrow environmental side issue.
- Annual emissions are flow variables, but atmospheric accumulation creates stock dynamics.
- Decarbonization depends on both activity levels and emissions intensity.
- Price instruments are useful but insufficient without infrastructure, standards, investment, and public capacity.
- Carbon lock-in slows transition through long-lived capital, incumbent interests, and inherited infrastructures.
- Adaptation and loss-and-damage are unavoidable parts of climate economics.
- Just transition is a legitimacy condition, not only a compensation program.
- Global cooperation depends on recognizing historical responsibility, unequal vulnerability, and development capacity.
- Implementation credibility is central to investment, household trust, and durable policy.

## Limitations

The workflow uses stylized synthetic data for teaching and article support. Applied analysis should extend it with greenhouse-gas inventories, sectoral energy balances, input-output tables, capital-stock data, climate-damage functions, vulnerability indices, household-energy-expenditure data, labor-market data, regional fiscal data, finance and cost-of-capital data, and international climate-finance datasets.
