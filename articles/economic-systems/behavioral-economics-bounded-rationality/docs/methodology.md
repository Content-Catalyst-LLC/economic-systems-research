# Methodology

## Purpose

This article companion models behavioral economics and bounded rationality as problems of limited attention, costly search, framing, reference points, time inconsistency, cognitive burden, default effects, social influence, and institutional choice architecture.

The workflow:

1. Creates stylized choice options with utility, complexity, salience, and search order.
2. Compares global utility maximization with satisficing under search constraints.
3. Models present bias using quasi-hyperbolic discounting.
4. Creates a loss-aversion value function around a reference point.
5. Models probability weighting under risk.
6. Simulates default effects and administrative burden in take-up decisions.
7. Models social-norm influence on compliance and cooperation.
8. Exports tables, figures, and a SQLite database.
9. Replicates the logic across Python, R, Stata, SQL, and Julia.

## Key Concepts

- Actors often satisfice rather than optimize because search and computation are costly.
- Present bias can make delayed benefits systematically undervalued.
- Loss aversion makes welfare evaluation asymmetric around reference points.
- Probability weighting can distort perceived risk.
- Defaults and framing matter because decision environments are never neutral.
- Administrative burden can reduce take-up even when formal eligibility and benefit are high.
- Social norms shape compliance, cooperation, public-goods behavior, and legitimacy.
- Behavioral economics complements institutional and structural analysis; it should not psychologize material deprivation or political inequality.

## Limitations

The workflow uses stylized synthetic data for teaching and article support. Applied analysis should extend it with survey data, field-experiment data, administrative data, randomized evaluations, household finance data, public-program take-up data, or behavioral-lab data.
