# Methodology

## Purpose

This article companion models circular economy and regenerative production as interacting material-flow, design, durability, repair, reuse, remanufacturing, recycling, residual-waste, regenerative-balance, infrastructure, labor, business-model, rebound, governance, justice, and resilience systems.

The workflow:

1. Creates stylized circular-material scenarios.
2. Calculates circularity ratios, virgin-input shares, residual waste, and waste-reduction ratios.
3. Models product-life extension and replacement-pressure reduction.
4. Compares repair, reuse, remanufacturing, recycling, and disposal pathways by value retention and ecological burden.
5. Scores design for durability, modularity, repairability, disassembly, and material separability.
6. Evaluates regenerative production through restoration, degradation, soil health, biodiversity, water retention, and local capability.
7. Scores circular infrastructure, reverse logistics, standards, and public procurement capacity.
8. Evaluates labor, skill, repair capacity, local service economies, and stewardship work.
9. Evaluates product-service systems, take-back responsibility, and ownership incentives.
10. Evaluates rebound risk and scale discipline.
11. Evaluates circular justice and burden distribution.
12. Exports tables, figures, and a SQLite database.
13. Replicates the logic across Python, R, Stata, SQL, and Julia.

## Key Concepts

- Linear systems are organized around extraction, production, use, and disposal.
- Circular systems preserve material value through durability, maintenance, repair, reuse, remanufacturing, recycling, and redesign.
- Regenerative systems seek active renewal of ecological and social systems.
- Recycling is useful but insufficient when used as an end-of-pipe substitute for design change.
- Product life extension can reduce replacement pressure and embodied material loss.
- Repair and remanufacturing preserve more value than low-quality recycling or disposal.
- Circularity must be judged by absolute throughput, not only relative recovery rates.
- Rebound effects can reduce the environmental benefits of efficiency and circular design.
- Circular systems require infrastructure, standards, public policy, skilled labor, and governance.
- Circular justice asks who benefits from circular systems and who bears extraction, waste, and transition burdens.

## Limitations

The workflow uses stylized synthetic data for teaching and article support. Applied analysis should extend it with product-level material-flow data, life-cycle assessment data, waste-management statistics, repair-market data, remanufacturing data, embodied-energy estimates, product-lifetime data, industrial-symbiosis datasets, soil and land-restoration metrics, household access data, and circular-policy indicators.
