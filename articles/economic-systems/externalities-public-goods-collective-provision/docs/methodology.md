# Methodology

## Purpose

This article companion models externalities, public goods, and collective provision as problems of social-cost accounting, underprovision, contribution incentives, burden distribution, and institutional capacity.

The workflow:

1. Creates stylized marginal private cost, marginal external cost, marginal private benefit, and marginal external benefit scenarios.
2. Calculates private and social optima for output.
3. Measures underprovision of public goods when voluntary contribution is lower than social need.
4. Models free-rider behavior using contribution scenarios.
5. Calculates collective provision capacity from taxation, borrowing, and pooled funds.
6. Distributes external costs across household groups and regions.
7. Simulates accumulated damage relative to ecological or infrastructural thresholds.
8. Exports tables, figures, and a SQLite database.
9. Replicates the logic across Python, R, Stata, SQL, and Julia.

## Key Concepts

- Externalities create gaps between private decisions and social consequences.
- Public goods are vulnerable to underprovision because benefits are diffuse and exclusion is difficult.
- Free riding is a structural incentive problem, not merely a moral failing.
- Collective provision requires organized financing, governance, monitoring, legitimacy, and burden sharing.
- Ecological and infrastructural thresholds make delay costly because some damages become irreversible or nonlinear.
- Distribution matters because the actors who create externalities are often not the same actors who bear the harm.

## Limitations

The workflow uses stylized synthetic data for teaching and article support. Applied analysis should extend it with environmental accounts, health data, infrastructure indicators, public-finance data, emissions inventories, household exposure data, and policy-specific cost-benefit estimates.
