# Methodology

## Purpose

This article companion models the future of economic systems in an age of limits as a set of interacting ecological, institutional, financial, technological, distributive, resilience, legitimacy, and measurement systems.

The workflow:

1. Creates stylized future economic-system scenarios.
2. Scores future viability using ecological pressure, institutional capacity, social inclusion, and resilience.
3. Calculates throughput pressure using population, affluence, and material intensity.
4. Scores transition capacity using public investment, policy credibility, technology, social trust, and implementation capacity.
5. Scores well-being beyond output using health, security, inclusion, ecological quality, public goods, care, and time.
6. Evaluates finance direction across fossil exposure, resilience investment, restoration, public goods, and short-termism.
7. Evaluates circularity, repair, maintenance, and regenerative capacity.
8. Scores technology governance by distribution, public purpose, capability, and ecological demand.
9. Scores global justice and asymmetric obligation.
10. Scores democratic transition legitimacy.
11. Exports tables, figures, and a SQLite database.
12. Replicates the logic across Python, R, Stata, SQL, and Julia.

## Key Concepts

- The future of economic systems must be evaluated by viability, not output alone.
- Ecological pressure and institutional capacity must be read together.
- Resource use depends on population, affluence, and intensity.
- Transition capacity requires public investment, credibility, technology, trust, and implementation.
- Measurement beyond GDP is central to future governance.
- Finance builds either stranded assets and fragility or durable infrastructure and resilience.
- Circularity, repair, and maintenance are not peripheral; they are system-design principles.
- Technology must be governed for capability, distribution, and ecological compatibility.
- Global obligation is asymmetric because responsibility and development need are unequal.
- Democratic legitimacy is a condition of transition durability.

## Limitations

The workflow uses stylized synthetic data for teaching and article support. Applied analysis should extend it with material-flow accounts, energy-system data, national accounts, household surveys, climate-risk data, infrastructure data, public-finance data, investment data, well-being indicators, circular-economy metrics, technology-adoption data, and global equity indicators.
