# Methodology

## Purpose

This article companion models natural capital, resource use, and environmental constraint as interacting stock-flow, extraction, regeneration, depletion, renewable-resource, nonrenewable-resource, waste-sink, ecosystem-function, mineral-energy, commons-governance, burden-distribution, resilience, and strategic-dependence systems.

The workflow:

1. Creates stylized natural-capital stock-flow scenarios.
2. Calculates next-period natural-capital stocks, regeneration gaps, depletion rates, and stock-risk indicators.
3. Evaluates resource-use ratios relative to regenerative capacity.
4. Evaluates waste constraints relative to absorptive capacity.
5. Models sector resource pressure across agriculture, forestry, mining, energy, construction, industry, transport, and public systems.
6. Scores ecosystem functions including water regulation, soil protection, pollination, carbon storage, habitat support, and flood buffering.
7. Compares renewable and nonrenewable resource regimes.
8. Scores substitution, efficiency, and rebound-risk conditions.
9. Evaluates resource justice and ecological-burden distribution.
10. Evaluates commons and property-regime governance.
11. Scores resilience and strategic dependence.
12. Exports tables, figures, and a SQLite database.
13. Replicates the logic across Python, R, Stata, SQL, and Julia.

## Key Concepts

- Natural capital includes ecological stocks and life-supporting functions.
- Resource use can draw from renewable flows or liquidate underlying stocks.
- Environmental constraint includes regenerative limits, absorptive limits, thresholds, and irreversibility.
- Renewable does not mean inexhaustible.
- Nonrenewable does not mean useless, but it requires strategic use, reuse, substitution, and restraint.
- Waste constraints matter as much as extraction constraints.
- Ecosystem functions are economically foundational even when poorly priced.
- Resource burdens are distributed unequally.
- Commons governance can outperform both unmanaged access and narrow privatization under the right conditions.
- Resilience depends on diversity, regeneration, redundancy, maintenance, and governance.

## Limitations

The workflow uses stylized synthetic data for teaching and article support. Applied analysis should extend it with material-flow accounts, natural-capital accounts, satellite land-use data, watershed data, resource-reserve data, environmental accounts, emissions inventories, soil and forest data, energy balances, mineral supply-chain data, ecosystem-service assessments, and household/environmental justice data.
