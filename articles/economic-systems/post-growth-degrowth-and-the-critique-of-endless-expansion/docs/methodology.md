# Methodology

## Purpose

This article companion models post-growth and degrowth as interacting growth-dependence, throughput, wellbeing, sufficiency, work-time, public-goods, finance, decoupling, justice, and transition-legitimacy systems.

The workflow:

1. Creates stylized post-growth and degrowth scenarios.
2. Scores growth dependence across employment, debt, fiscal stability, asset valuation, pensions, housing, and political legitimacy.
3. Calculates throughput using population, affluence, and material or energy intensity.
4. Scores multidimensional wellbeing using health, time, security, equality, public goods, ecological quality, care support, and trust.
5. Calculates sufficiency as needs met per unit of throughput required.
6. Models shorter working time, labor security, and care.
7. Evaluates public-goods and social infrastructure under lower material pressure.
8. Models finance and debt dependence on expansion.
9. Evaluates decoupling and rebound scenarios.
10. Scores degrowth transition credibility using throughput reduction, redistribution, public services, democratic legitimacy, and macro-stabilization.
11. Evaluates global justice and asymmetric responsibility across country groups.
12. Exports tables, figures, and a SQLite database.
13. Replicates the logic across Python, R, Stata, SQL, and Julia.

## Key Concepts

- Post-growth decouples prosperity from GDP growth as the overriding objective.
- Degrowth argues for planned reductions of energy and material throughput in high-income economies.
- Growth dependence is institutional, not merely ideological.
- Wellbeing is multidimensional and cannot be reduced to output.
- Total throughput depends on scale, not only efficiency.
- Sufficiency asks how well needs are met relative to material pressure.
- Degrowth is not recession; it requires planning, redistribution, public goods, and legitimacy.
- Decoupling debates turn on absolute reductions, speed, scale, and rebound.
- Global justice requires distinguishing excess consumption from unmet development need.
- Any credible post-growth transition must answer macroeconomic stability questions directly.

## Limitations

The workflow uses stylized synthetic data for teaching and article support. Applied analysis should extend it with national accounts, material-flow accounts, energy-use data, household-expenditure data, time-use surveys, wellbeing indicators, debt and financial-stability data, fiscal data, labor-market data, public-goods indicators, inequality datasets, emissions inventories, and country-level development indicators.
