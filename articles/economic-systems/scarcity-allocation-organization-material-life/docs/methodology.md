# Methodology

## Purpose

This article companion represents scarcity as a multi-dimensional allocation problem rather than a simple textbook mismatch between unlimited wants and limited means.

The workflow:

1. Creates stylized public-priority categories.
2. Builds baseline and alternative allocation scenarios.
3. Calculates allocation shifts, future-capacity shares, public-provision shares, and restoration shares.
4. Creates household-level effective-access examples based on need, income, price, and institutional provision.
5. Computes distribution-weighted access and deprivation indicators.
6. Simulates intertemporal allocation among consumption, investment, maintenance, and ecological restoration.
7. Models ecological use against regenerative capacity.
8. Exports tables, figures, and a SQLite database.
9. Replicates the logic across Python, R, Stata, SQL, and Julia.

## Key Concepts

- Scarcity is physical, institutional, distributional, temporal, and ecological.
- Allocation is a public and institutional ordering of priorities.
- Effective demand and human need are not the same thing.
- Maintenance and restoration are not optional extras; they preserve future capacity.
- Ecological scarcity appears when use or burden exceeds regenerative or absorptive capacity.
- Distribution-weighted analysis can reveal allocation failures hidden by aggregate totals.

## Limitations

The workflow uses stylized synthetic data for teaching and article support. It should be extended with real budgets, household microdata, ecological indicators, and public-finance data for applied policy analysis.
