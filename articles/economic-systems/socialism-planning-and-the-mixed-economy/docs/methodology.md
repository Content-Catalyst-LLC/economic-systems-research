# Methodology

## Purpose

This article companion models socialism, planning, and the mixed economy as interacting market-allocation, public-provision, ownership, regulatory, social-rights, industrial-policy, public-utility, welfare-state, planning-capacity, crisis-response, globalization, and sustainability systems.

The workflow:

1. Creates stylized mixed-economy structures and sectoral coordination scenarios.
2. Calculates market-allocation, public-provision, regulation, social-rights, and public-ownership indicators.
3. Scores planning capacity using state capacity, data quality, institutional reach, feedback, and democratic accountability.
4. Evaluates decommodification through public services, social insurance, guaranteed access, and affordability.
5. Compares sectoral fit for markets, planning, public ownership, cooperative provision, and regulation.
6. Models public utilities and natural monopoly governance.
7. Assesses industrial-policy and infrastructure coordination.
8. Evaluates crisis coordination and sustainable-transition coordination.
9. Examines globalization constraints on national planning capacity.
10. Exports tables, figures, and a SQLite database.
11. Replicates the logic across Python, R, Stata, SQL, and Julia.

## Key Concepts

- Real economies are mixed rather than pure.
- Planning and markets are both coordination mechanisms, not moral absolutes.
- Socialism asks who owns, who controls, and what production is for.
- Public ownership is strongest where universal access, natural monopoly, public investment, and long time horizons matter.
- Planning capacity depends on information, institutions, feedback, legitimacy, and accountability.
- Decommodification changes how households experience risk and necessity.
- Industrial policy and infrastructure planning are normal features of complex economies.
- Crisis coordination often depends on public capacity more than decentralized adjustment.
- Ecological transition requires long-horizon planning, investment coordination, and social protection.
- Mixed economies should be judged by fit, capability, resilience, legitimacy, and sustainability.

## Limitations

The workflow uses stylized synthetic data for teaching and article support. Applied analysis should extend it with public-sector accounts, national accounts, social spending data, public investment datasets, state-owned enterprise data, utility regulation, industrial-policy programs, welfare-state indicators, public-service access, crisis-response data, climate-investment plans, and governance indicators.
