# Methodology

## Purpose

This article companion models beyond-GDP measurement as an interacting system of national accounting, well-being, inclusion, distribution, sustainability stocks, care, subjective well-being, capability conversion, ecological pressure, indicator design, and governance credibility.

The workflow:

1. Creates stylized GDP and dashboard scenarios.
2. Calculates GDP using the expenditure identity.
3. Scores multidimensional well-being across health, education, income security, housing, safety, social connection, environment, and time balance.
4. Scores inclusion using distribution, mobility, access, voice, regional equity, and service reach.
5. Scores sustainability using natural capital, human capital, institutional trust, produced capital, ecological pressure, and maintenance.
6. Calculates adjusted progress by subtracting social and ecological costs from current benefits.
7. Models care visibility and unpaid labor contributions.
8. Models subjective well-being and objective indicator divergence.
9. Compares dashboards and composite-index weighting choices.
10. Evaluates perverse incentives and ranking risk.
11. Exports tables, figures, and a SQLite database.
12. Replicates the logic across Python, R, Stata, SQL, and Julia.

## Key Concepts

- GDP is an activity measure, not a complete welfare measure.
- Well-being is multidimensional and cannot be reduced to output.
- Inclusion requires distribution, access, mobility, and voice.
- Sustainability requires maintaining stocks that support future life.
- Capabilities depend on resources, public goods, and conversion conditions.
- Care and unpaid labor are economically foundational but often weakly counted.
- Subjective well-being complements objective indicators.
- Dashboards preserve nuance; composite indices communicate summary judgments.
- Measurement systems can improve governance or produce perverse incentives.
- Indicators should inform public reasoning, not replace it.

## Limitations

The workflow uses stylized synthetic data for teaching and article support. Applied analysis should extend it with national accounts, household surveys, labor-force data, time-use data, subjective well-being surveys, health and education data, environmental accounts, natural-capital accounts, fiscal data, regional inequality indicators, social mobility data, and administrative service-access data.
