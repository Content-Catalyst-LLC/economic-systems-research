# Methodology

## Purpose

This article companion models economic resilience, fragility, and adaptive capacity as interacting systems of buffers, redundancy, exposure, leverage, concentration, household security, firm capacity, public coordination, infrastructure interdependence, finance, labor adaptation, ecological risk, trust, information, recovery, and transformation.

The workflow:

1. Creates stylized resilience, fragility, adaptive-capacity, and shock scenarios.
2. Scores resilience using buffers, redundancy, coordination, trust, learning, and recovery capacity.
3. Scores fragility using leverage, concentration, exposure, underinvestment, inequality, and political fragmentation.
4. Models shock impact as shock magnitude multiplied by vulnerability.
5. Evaluates household and firm resilience across income security, savings, care, housing, credit, suppliers, and liquidity.
6. Evaluates infrastructure and supply-chain interdependence.
7. Scores financial fragility through leverage, refinancing risk, asset dependence, and contagion channels.
8. Scores labor-market adaptive capacity through skills, retraining, income support, mobility, and place-based investment.
9. Scores ecological and energy vulnerability.
10. Evaluates recovery, learning, and transformational adaptation.
11. Exports tables, figures, and a SQLite database.
12. Replicates the logic across Python, R, Stata, SQL, and Julia.

## Key Concepts

- Resilience is not only rebound; it includes absorption, continuity, recovery, adaptation, and transformation.
- Fragility is often built into ordinary systems before crises arrive.
- Adaptive capacity is unevenly distributed across households, firms, regions, and institutions.
- Over-optimization can make systems brittle by removing buffers and redundancy.
- Public institutions are resilience infrastructure.
- Finance can buffer shocks or amplify fragility.
- Labor-market resilience requires skills, income support, and credible transition pathways.
- Ecological risk is economic risk because economies depend on material and environmental foundations.
- Trust and information affect the politics of crisis response.
- Measuring resilience requires multidimensional indicators rather than one comforting ranking.

## Limitations

The workflow uses stylized synthetic data for teaching and article support. Applied analysis should extend it with national accounts, disaster-loss data, household balance sheets, firm financials, supply-chain data, infrastructure data, labor-force data, public-health capacity, fiscal data, climate-risk indicators, insurance data, and regional resilience metrics.
