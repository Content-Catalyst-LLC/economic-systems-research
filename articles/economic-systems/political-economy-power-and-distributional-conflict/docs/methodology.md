# Methodology

## Purpose

This article companion models political economy, power, and distributional conflict as interacting income-share, bargaining, fiscal, debt, rent, welfare, globalization, crisis, institutional-representation, and legitimacy systems.

The workflow:

1. Creates stylized distributional scenarios for wages, profits, rents, taxes, transfers, and household exposure.
2. Calculates wage shares, profit shares, rent shares, and net fiscal positions.
3. Scores bargaining power using ownership, organization, access, mobility, voice, and institutional reach.
4. Models capital-labor conflict and real-income pressure under inflation.
5. Evaluates creditor-debtor relations through debt-service pressure and fiscal-obligation effects.
6. Evaluates rent extraction through housing, monopoly, platform, and intellectual-property scenarios.
7. Evaluates welfare-state solidarity and social-rights buffering.
8. Models globalization exposure and cross-border bargaining constraints.
9. Evaluates crisis burden-sharing and who absorbs losses.
10. Scores legitimacy through fairness, security, voice, institutional trust, and representation.
11. Exports tables, figures, and a SQLite database.
12. Replicates the logic across Python, R, Stata, SQL, and Julia.

## Key Concepts

- Markets are institutionally built rather than politically neutral.
- Distribution concerns wages, profits, rents, taxes, transfers, public goods, and risk exposure.
- Power operates through ownership, bargaining, law, finance, access, mobility, media, and institutional voice.
- Fiscal systems express political choices about who pays, who receives, and what the state protects.
- Inflation redistributes real income and reveals conflict over adjustment.
- Debt creates enforceable claims on future household, firm, and public resources.
- Rents and ownership power can concentrate gains without equivalent productive contribution.
- Welfare states mediate distributional conflict through social rights and risk pooling.
- Globalization changes domestic bargaining power by expanding exit options and external constraints.
- Crisis reveals who is protected, who waits, and who pays.
- Legitimacy depends on whether institutions mediate conflict credibly and visibly.

## Limitations

The workflow uses stylized synthetic data for teaching and article support. Applied analysis should extend it with national accounts, distributional national accounts, labor-force surveys, union and collective-bargaining data, tax-benefit microdata, household debt data, housing-cost data, firm markups, wealth data, lobbying data, campaign finance data, social spending data, inflation and wage data, and political-representation indicators.
