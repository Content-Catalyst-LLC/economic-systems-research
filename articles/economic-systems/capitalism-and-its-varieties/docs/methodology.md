# Methodology

## Purpose

This article companion models capitalism and its varieties as interacting labor, finance, welfare, corporate-governance, innovation, housing, globalization, crisis-response, inequality, and sustainability systems.

The workflow:

1. Creates stylized comparative-capitalism regimes and scenario datasets.
2. Calculates profit, wage share, profit share, finance intensity, welfare-buffer strength, labor coordination, institutional advantage, housing vulnerability, crisis-response capacity, inequality pressure, and sustainable-capitalism scores.
3. Compares liberal, coordinated, developmental, finance-led, welfare, resource-dependent, and hybrid capitalisms.
4. Evaluates institutional complementarities among labor regimes, finance type, training systems, firm coordination, welfare institutions, and public capacity.
5. Distinguishes productive investment from shareholder extraction and financialized accumulation.
6. Models how housing and household debt transmit capitalist variety into everyday security.
7. Assesses crisis response and adaptive capacity.
8. Exports tables, figures, and a SQLite database.
9. Replicates the logic across Python, R, Stata, SQL, and Julia.

## Key Concepts

- Capitalism is not one model; it is a family of historically specific institutional orders.
- Profit depends on revenue, cost, finance, demand, labor institutions, and public infrastructure.
- Wage shares and profit shares differ across institutional regimes.
- Financialization changes time horizons and governance priorities.
- Welfare systems buffer households from capitalist risk.
- Labor regimes and training systems shape productive capabilities.
- Corporate governance affects distribution, investment, and firm time horizons.
- Housing regimes connect finance, property, household vulnerability, and inequality.
- Global value chains produce hybrid and dependent forms of capitalism.
- Crisis response reveals institutional priorities and resilience.
- Sustainable evaluation must include inequality, legitimacy, ecological constraint, and public purpose.

## Limitations

The workflow uses stylized synthetic data for teaching and article support. Applied analysis should extend it with national accounts, firm-level data, labor-market indicators, wage-share data, union density, collective bargaining coverage, financial accounts, household debt, social spending, housing-cost data, value-chain indicators, emissions data, and institutional datasets.
