# Methodology

## Purpose

This article companion models industrial policy and the developmental state as interacting sectoral, financial, infrastructural, labor, technological, territorial, governance, and ecological systems.

The workflow:

1. Creates stylized strategic-sector, support-conditionality, development-finance, infrastructure-energy, skills-labor, capture-risk, regional-cluster, and green-industrial-policy scenarios.
2. Calculates sectoral output shares, sectoral productivity, export ratios, support intensity, and strategic priority scores.
3. Evaluates whether public support is linked to productivity, exports, employment, domestic linkages, and emissions reduction.
4. Measures the alignment of development finance with long-horizon industrial capability.
5. Evaluates infrastructure and energy readiness for industrial upgrading.
6. Scores skills and labor systems for upgrading potential.
7. Identifies capture, rent-seeking, and policy-failure risks.
8. Evaluates regional cluster depth and territorial inclusion.
9. Assesses green industrial policy across productivity, emissions, employment, resilience, and domestic linkage.
10. Exports tables, figures, and a SQLite database.
11. Replicates the logic across Python, R, Stata, SQL, and Julia.

## Key Concepts

- Industrial policy is strategic public action to shape productive structure, not subsidy alone.
- The developmental state is an institutional capacity, not merely a large public sector.
- Markets often allocate within existing structures; industrial policy addresses how structures change.
- Learning spillovers and coordination failures can justify public strategy.
- Public support is stronger when tied to performance conditionality.
- Development finance can redirect investment toward long-horizon productive capability.
- Infrastructure, energy, and skills are material preconditions of industrial upgrading.
- Capture risk must be actively monitored.
- Green industrial policy must align decarbonization with productivity, labor, resilience, and domestic capability.

## Limitations

The workflow uses stylized synthetic data for teaching and article support. Applied analysis should extend it with sectoral output data, firm-level productivity data, export data, subsidy records, procurement data, development-bank lending data, skills and labor statistics, supplier network data, regional infrastructure data, energy-reliability indicators, emissions data, and policy evaluation records.
