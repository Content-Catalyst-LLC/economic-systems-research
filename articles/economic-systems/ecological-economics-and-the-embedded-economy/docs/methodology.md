# Methodology

## Purpose

This article companion models ecological economics and the embedded economy as interacting throughput, scale, waste, material footprint, ecological-capacity, care, institutional, commons, burden-distribution, resilience, and post-growth systems.

The workflow:

1. Creates stylized ecological-economy scenarios for energy input, material input, recovery, ecological capacity, and wellbeing.
2. Calculates throughput, ecological scale ratios, waste residuals, recovery rates, and overshoot risk.
3. Models sectoral material and energy footprints across food, housing, transport, manufacturing, digital infrastructure, care, and public services.
4. Evaluates strong sustainability and non-substitutable natural-capital risk.
5. Scores ecological burden using exposure, income, infrastructure, adaptive capacity, and historical responsibility.
6. Scores social embeddedness using care capacity, public services, institutional trust, infrastructure, and cultural reciprocity.
7. Compares commons governance arrangements using monitoring, rules, participation, sanctions, and regeneration.
8. Evaluates resilience through diversity, redundancy, regeneration, maintenance, governance, and learning.
9. Models growth, efficiency, rebound, steady-state, degrowth, and post-growth scenarios.
10. Exports tables, figures, and a SQLite database.
11. Replicates the logic across Python, R, Stata, SQL, and Julia.

## Key Concepts

- The economy is a subsystem of the biosphere.
- Economic activity is physical as well as monetary.
- Throughput is the flow of energy and materials through production and consumption.
- Scale matters relative to ecological capacity.
- Waste is intrinsic to production, even when recovery improves.
- Strong sustainability treats some ecological functions as non-substitutable.
- Care, institutions, and public infrastructure are economic foundations, not background.
- Ecological burdens are socially distributed.
- Commons require governance, not simple privatization or unmanaged open access.
- Resilience depends on diversity, redundancy, regeneration, governance, maintenance, and learning.
- Post-growth analysis asks how wellbeing can be protected without endless material expansion.

## Limitations

The workflow uses stylized synthetic data for teaching and article support. Applied analysis should extend it with national material-flow accounts, energy balances, environmental input-output tables, ecosystem-service assessments, household expenditure data, emissions inventories, water data, land-use data, care-work surveys, infrastructure access data, and social vulnerability indicators.
