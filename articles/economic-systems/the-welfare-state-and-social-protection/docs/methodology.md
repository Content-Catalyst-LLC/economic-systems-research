# Methodology

## Purpose

This article companion models welfare states and social protection as interacting tax-transfer, social-insurance, social-assistance, universal-service, fiscal, administrative, care, labor-market, life-course, and resilience systems.

The workflow:

1. Creates stylized household, program, regime, life-course, care, unemployment, pension, healthcare, fiscal-capacity, targeting/universalism, digital-administration, climate-shock, and adaptive-protection scenarios.
2. Calculates social spending ratios, disposable income, coverage rates, replacement rates, and vulnerability scores.
3. Compares social insurance, social assistance, and universal provision.
4. Evaluates benefit adequacy and coverage gaps.
5. Models unemployment, sickness, disability, care, old age, and family risk.
6. Assesses targeting, stigma, administrative burden, take-up, and universalism.
7. Evaluates digital administration, automation, error risk, and access burden.
8. Models adaptive social-protection scaling under climate and crisis shocks.
9. Exports tables, figures, and a SQLite database.
10. Replicates the logic across Python, R, Stata, SQL, and Julia.

## Key Concepts

- The welfare state is social infrastructure, not merely public spending.
- Social protection redistributes risk as well as income.
- Coverage and adequacy matter as much as program existence.
- Replacement rates shape continuity during unemployment, sickness, old age, and disability.
- Targeting can direct resources but may create stigma, exclusion, and administrative burden.
- Universal provision can build dignity, simplicity, and political durability.
- Fiscal capacity links taxation, administration, and benefit quality.
- Digital administration can widen access or automate exclusion.
- Adaptive social protection is central to climate resilience and crisis response.
- Welfare-state analysis must evaluate dignity, participation, resilience, and capability, not spending alone.

## Limitations

The workflow uses stylized synthetic data for teaching and article support. Applied analysis should extend it with national accounts, social spending data, tax-benefit microdata, benefit eligibility records, administrative data, labor-force data, pension and healthcare indicators, household surveys, digital-access records, disaster exposure data, and social-protection payment systems.
