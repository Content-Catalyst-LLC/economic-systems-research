# Methodology

## Purpose

This article companion models inequality, redistribution, and social mobility as interacting income, wealth, tax-transfer, public-service, labor-market, housing, education-health, place, social-insurance, and intergenerational systems.

The workflow:

1. Creates stylized household distribution, wealth, tax-transfer, public-service, labor-market, housing-place, education-health, mobility, and security scenarios.
2. Calculates income shares, cumulative shares, simple Gini-style inequality, and top/bottom ratios.
3. Compares market income with disposable income after taxes, transfers, and public-service value.
4. Measures wealth concentration and intergenerational asset transfer.
5. Scores opportunity through education, health, wealth, place, and networks.
6. Models intergenerational persistence through a simple parent-child outcome relation.
7. Evaluates housing-cost burden and place-based opportunity.
8. Assesses labor-market bargaining and earnings dispersion.
9. Evaluates social insurance and economic security.
10. Exports tables, figures, and a SQLite database.
11. Replicates the logic across Python, R, Stata, SQL, and Julia.

## Key Concepts

- Income is a flow; wealth is a stock.
- Market income differs from disposable income after taxes, transfers, and public services.
- Wealth concentration can reproduce advantage across generations.
- Mobility depends on institutions, not aspiration alone.
- Housing and place organize opportunity spatially.
- Labor-market bargaining power shapes earnings dispersion.
- Social insurance redistributes risk as well as money.
- Public services can be deeply redistributive even when universally provided.
- Inequality is a systems question of income, wealth, security, public capacity, power, and future possibility.

## Limitations

The workflow uses stylized synthetic data for teaching and article support. Applied analysis should extend it with household survey microdata, tax records, wealth distribution data, mobility panels, school-quality indicators, health data, housing-cost data, regional labor-market data, public-service valuation, and social-insurance records.
